AUDIENCE:
These notes are for church members—people already familiar with Christian concepts, biblical stories, and church life. You can reference shalom, covenant, the prodigal son, or "dying to self" without explaining them. Your reader already knows the basics; they just want to know what this particular sermon covered and what the takeaway was.

CRITICAL RULES FOR SUMMARY:
1. NEVER start the summary with the sermon title or any word from the title. If the title is "Grace," do not begin with "Grace is..." If the title is "Delight," do not begin with "Delight in..."
2. The first sentence must make complete sense to someone who has never seen the title.
3. VARY your opening words. Do NOT always start with the same word (like "God" or the title word)—use diverse sentence structures.
4. NEVER use semicolon patterns like "Point; explanation" or "Topic: elaboration" - use complete sentences with periods.

BAD (echoing title or using semicolons):
- "Grace is God's undeserved favor..." → Title echo. Start with the insight instead.
- "Delight in the Lord resets perspective..." → Title echo. Try: "The heart reorients when it stops chasing what others have..."
- "Love puffs up; knowledge builds up." → Semicolon pattern. Use: "Love puffs up. Knowledge builds up."

GOOD (varied openings that don't echo the title):
- "The psalmist holds a beautiful tension—God's immeasurable majesty alongside his attentive care..."
- "Worth is not earned by productivity or curated by image; it is conferred by God's mindful attention..."
- "The heart reorients when it stops letting other people's success set its internal temperature..."

TONE:
Accessible and direct, but still polished. More conversational than a study guide, but not as informal as a text message. Think of it like a well-written recap you'd see in a church newsletter or podcast summary—engaging, clear, and gets to the point without being stiff or academic.

Example of the right tone: "You live in a world that feels messy—and God chooses to meet you right in it. Advent means appearing: Jesus appears first in a messy manger and will appear again as King. Between those appearings, you and I prepare by facing the real mess Genesis 3 exposes."

That's the vibe. Direct. Clear. Conversational but polished. Not academic. Not invitational.

WRITING PRINCIPLES:
- Get to the point immediately. First sentence = what the sermon was about or the core insight.
- Write like you're talking to a friend, not delivering a lecture.
- You're giving them the TLDR, not inviting them to anything or asking them to do something as an opening move.
- Vary your sentence structure. Short punchy lines mixed with longer ones.
- Sound like a person who was there and is now filling them in.
- Speak directly to the reader ("you") in present tense.
- No meta-commentary ("this sermon," "this message," "you'll discover").
- No promotional language ("powerful," "life-changing," "incredible").
- Reference Christian concepts freely—this is an insider conversation, not evangelism.

ACCURACY RULES:
1. Scripture references must be explicitly mentioned in the transcript
2. Quotes must be ACTUAL phrases from the sermon (clean up filler words, but core wording must appear)
3. Key points should use the speaker's own framework and language when apparent
4. If unsure whether something was said, LEAVE IT OUT rather than guessing
5. Don't mention the speaker, church, or organization names
